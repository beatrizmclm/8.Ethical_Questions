ETHICAL QUESTIONS
Family dynamics, non-disclosure, autosomal dominant inheritance,
dealing with uncertainty and confidentiality
You receive a ward referral from oncology. Stephanie, aged 43, has been diagnosed with a terminal
metastatic bowel tumour and has one daughter and two sons aged 23, 25 and 27, respectively. You
decide to store a blood sample, but it becomes clear that a familial bowel cancer susceptibility gene
mutation (msh2) has been previously identified in her father, the result of which was never disclosed
to the rest of the family, despite previous discussions in genetic counselling.
1. Draw the pedigree
2. Would you disclose this previous result to Stephanie and her family?
There seems to be no consent to share this result with the family. Given the young age at which
HNPCC appears and its family history, you could recommend genetic testing for the MSH2 gene,
beginning with IHC testing.
3. If so, when and how would you do it?
When and how to disclose the results should be discussed with the family in private.
4. What are the pros and cons of confirming a genetic diagnosis?
Pros:
- Explains to the family as to why this cancer has developed.
- Confirms that the children are at a 50% risk.
Cons:
- This may worry the patient who may not have been aware that her children were at a high
risk of developing a tumour.

- The results may lead to anger and grief in the family, with little time or opportunity to

5. Who has ownership of the previous result?
The father has a right not to disclose the result, even after he has died. Cause of death and death
certificates are public information and used for national statistics.
6. What are the rights of the deceased to keep their medical records confidential?
Medical records, such as histopathological reports, can be requested on the deceased by the
next of kin, but these will not be released if it is reasonably believed that the patient did not
wish to do so before they died.
7. Given limited resources, should you repeat all of the genetic tests without
searching only for the familial mutation?
The difference in cost of a targeted mutation screen compared with full sequencing can be over
1,000. Given a finite yearly budget, two options are available:
- Consider the test a positive control laboratory governance test.
- Using finite resources to offer full testing where a mutation result is available is unethical, as
other families will miss out on testing as a result.
Therefore, request only the targeted screen and only consider full testing if the mutation is not
identified i.e. a phenocopy is expected. In this way, the fathers genetic result will not be
disclosed.
Ethical Principle The greater good to the whole population out-weighs any low risk level of
harm to the reputation of the deceased.
8. Would your decision be influenced if one of the sons had an altered bowel
habit for the past three weeks?
In this scenario, the result could still not be given to the family but the importance of clarifying
the likelihood of finding an underlying malignancy with an urgent targeted laboratory screen
could be justified.

Pattern recognition, childhood testing and autosomal dominant
inheritance
David, who is married with one five-year-old son, calls the genetics department complaining of
recurrent morning headaches, vomiting and dizziness. Previous tests have shown that he has a
retinal angioma. His father, James, died at the age of 50 after treatment for brain and renal
tumours.
On examination, David has evidence of cerebellar ataxia and blurred optic discs. He is worried as
well about the risk to his five-year-old son.
1. Draw the pedigree
2. What is the most likely cause of his symptoms?
Cerebellar haemangioblastoma
3. What is the most appropriate test?
Brain+ spinal cord MRI
4. What simple bedside measurement should be recorded?
Blood pressure
5. What is the underlying genetic diagnosis?
Von Hippel-Lindau syndrome
6. What is the probability that Davids son is also a mutation carrier and at what
age would you test him?
One in 2 as likely this is an autosomal dominant pedigree. Test at age five to start ophthalmological,
urine and blood pressure monitoring if the familial gene mutation is identified.

7. Why would some parents be reluctant to consent their child to genetic testing?
Avoid genetic label, concerns about insurance, keen to involve child in testing decision, social
understanding of genetic disease and not wanting to face up to the problem
8. What are the pros and cons of testing?
Pros: Avoidance of unnecessary screening and reassurance, concentrating resources where required.
Cons: No preventable treatment available only surgery for early stage disease.
9. How could these issues be resolved?
It is critical to engage couple in genetic counselling. Try to understand concerns and keep family on
side as much as possible - A loss of relationship and involving social services has to be a last resort.
Discuss with GP and use organisations/information available such as contact a family.
